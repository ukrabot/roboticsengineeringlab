Universal Robots UR20: Full Review of the 20 kg Cobot

What the UR20 Is and Why It Matters in 2026

The UR20 is the highest-payload cobot Universal Robots has released to date in its standard lineup, alongside the UR30. It launched in 2022 as the first model built on a new joint architecture, and has since become a common choice for palletizing, machine tending, and inspection in mid-sized North American factories.

Unlike a generic robot-arm overview, this review focuses on the numbers that actually change a purchasing decision: rated payload versus payload under boundary conditions, five-year total cost of ownership, and which safety standards apply depending on the intended use case.

Verified Technical Specifications

The UR20 has a reach of 1750 mm and a rated payload of 20 kg, expandable to 25 kg under boundary conditions defined by the manufacturer (for example, with lighter grippers or a center of mass closer to the wrist flange). It weighs 64 kg and its base has a footprint of just 245 mm in diameter, letting it fit into tight spaces despite its long reach.

Collaborative Safety: What It Actually Covers

The UR20 includes 21 configurable safety functions and an integrated wrist force/torque sensor, complying with EN ISO 13849-1 (PLd, Category 3), EN ISO 10218-1, and UL 1740. This makes it possible, in principle, to run without a dedicated safety cage in some scenarios. In practice, since it moves up to 20-25 kg, almost no real-world deployment skips additional safeguards entirely: ISO/TS 15066 still requires a site-specific risk assessment that accounts for speed, the attached tooling, and the type of contact an operator could have with the arm.

The short version: "collaborative" doesn't mean "no risk assessment." Any serious integrator will run impact-force testing, and in most palletizing or welding applications will still recommend some form of perimeter or speed limitation in shared zones.

Programming and Software Ecosystem

The UR20 is programmed through PolyScope 5, Universal Robots' graphical interface that supports hand-guiding — physically moving the arm to record points — without writing code. For more advanced integrations, it supports Python, C++, and native ROS 2 compatibility, making it easier to connect with machine-vision systems or custom workflows. This dual path — a simple interface for basic tasks and an open API for integrators — is one of the reasons Universal Robots holds the largest global market share in cobots.

Price and Total Cost of Ownership

Universal Robots doesn't publish a single list price; cost depends on the distributor, region, and included accessories (gripper, base, safety kit). In North America, observed pricing from authorized distributors runs roughly $55,000-$65,000 for the arm with controller, excluding gripper, integration, and commissioning. Realistically, you should add:

  • End effector (gripper or tool): $2,000-$15,000 depending on the application.
  • Integration and initial programming: can match or exceed the price of the arm itself for a complex cell.
  • Operator training and preventive maintenance: a recurring annual cost many initial budgets leave out.
  • Risk assessment and any added safety hardware (area scanners, light curtains) if the application requires it.

A common budgeting mistake is comparing only the arm's price across vendors. The real cost of a palletizing or machine-tending cell built around a UR20 often runs two to three times the price of the robot alone once integration is added.

Where the UR20 Makes Sense

Given its combination of payload and reach, the UR20 fits especially well for palletizing medium-to-heavy boxes (its reach covers a full standard Euro pallet height from a single position), tending CNC or injection-molding machines, and inspecting mid-to-large parts. It's not the most cost-effective choice for light electronics work or precision assembly, where smaller models in the UR lineup — the UR10e or UR16e — are more economical.

Checklist Before Buying a UR20

  • Confirm your application's real payload, including the end effector's weight, and compare it against the 20 kg rated figure — not the 25 kg boundary-condition figure.
  • Ask your integrator for a site-specific risk assessment before assuming you won't need a safety perimeter.
  • Get the full cell price, not just the arm: gripper, integration, training, and 5-year maintenance.
  • Check the service network available in your region and typical response times for repairs.
  • Compare against other models in the same lineup (UR16e, UR30) if your actual payload sits near the UR20's lower or upper limit.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can the UR20 really carry 25 kg?

Yes, but only under the boundary conditions defined in the user manual (for example, a center of mass close to the wrist flange). For general application sizing, it's safer to plan around the 20 kg rated payload.

Do I need a safety cage for the UR20?

It depends on your specific installation's risk assessment — there's no single answer. Collaborative certification reduces requirements in many cases, but it doesn't remove them automatically, especially near maximum payload.

What's the difference between the UR20 and the UR20e?

They share the same rated payload and reach, but the "e" variant includes upgrades such as an IP65 protection rating versus the original version's IP54, useful in dusty or wet environments.
